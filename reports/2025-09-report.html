Historic Federal Reserve Pivot

The Federal Reserve cut interest rates by 0.25 percentage points at its mid-September meeting—the Fed's first rate cut since late 2024. This marks a remarkable turn from a year ago when inflation was the chief worry. The Fed now views inflation as under control, giving it room to shore up employment.

Economic Overview

Fed Delivers Long-Awaited Rate Relief

The Federal Reserve cut its policy rate to 4.00%-4.25% in response to a softening labor market and heightened recession risks. Fed Chair Jerome Powell emphasized that rising unemployment and slowing job creation had overtaken inflation as the Fed's primary concern. The decision was nearly unanimous, with only one governor dissenting in favor of an even larger cut.

New Fed projections show policymakers anticipate at least two more quarter-point cuts by year-end 2025, signaling a commitment to easing until the economy regains its footing. Powell noted that recent data confirms the labor market "has weakened at the margins" and that "we don't need it to soften anymore."

Labor Market at Stall Speed

The unemployment rate has drifted up to 4.4%, with underemployment metrics rising even faster. Job growth averaged a mere ~30,000 per month over the summer—essentially stall speed for the labor market. This dramatic cooling cemented the Fed's decision to act preemptively against a potential recession.

August's CPI showed inflation at approximately 2.9% year-over-year, a slight uptick mainly due to base effects and higher gasoline prices, but core inflation held around 3%. The Fed views the inflation pop from tariffs as temporary, with price pressures expected to fade after year-end even as policy loosens.

NYC Real Estate Analysis

Rental Market Shows Signs of Balance

The rental market, while still expensive, is showing clear signs of topping out. Manhattan's median rent in September remained roughly flat at around $4,600, and year-over-year rent inflation is decelerating to an expected 5-6%, down from the 8-10% gains of spring.

Brokers report that more landlords are offering small concessions again (one month free on select units)—something almost unheard of earlier in the summer—as vacancies inch up and renters push back on prices. The typical fall seasonal slowdown is reasserting itself after an abnormally intense summer.

Sales Market Awakens

The sales market has begun to stir in response to falling interest rates. Mortgage rates dropping under 6% for the first time in months released pent-up demand. Manhattan signed contract volume in September was up double-digits compared to a year ago, and open house traffic improved notably in both Manhattan and Brooklyn.

New Yorkers who had been delaying purchases due to high financing costs are now reengaging, encouraged by the Fed's clear shift. This reallocation from renting to owning could further soften rental demand going forward.

NYC Economy Holding Steady

NYC's broader economic health is holding up—the city's job count is above pre-pandemic highs and private-sector wages are finally outpacing inflation. Wall Street profits and bonuses, a key driver of the high-end real estate market, are projected to rise modestly this year, which bodes well for luxury sales and rentals.

Market Outlook

September 2025 marked a turning point: the Fed delivered the rate relief that markets had been craving, set to reverberate through housing and credit conditions in coming months. As one economist noted, the Fed is finally providing "the only medicine that can help" the ailing economy.

Implications for Market Participants

For Renters: Expect more breathing room. The era of relentless rent records is likely ending as supply increases and tenant budgets max out. Look for concessions and be prepared to negotiate, especially on longer lease terms.

For Buyers: Seize the opportunity of lower interest rates. Affordability, while still challenging in NYC, is improving at the margins for the first time in years. Lock in rates before potential market volatility.

For Sellers: The improved buyer pool should help move appropriately priced properties. Consider that further rate cuts could bring more buyers to the market through year-end.

Fall 2025 Forecast

We anticipate a relatively balanced fall season characterized by:

  • Modest rent declines or flat rents in many NYC neighborhoods
  • Slow but steady uptick in sales inventory and transactions
  • Continued Fed easing with likely cuts in October and December
  • Mortgage rates potentially falling toward 5.5% by year-end

Economic wild cards remain (trade war re-escalation or government shutdown could pose risks), but with inflation contained and the Fed firmly in easing mode, the outlook is much more favorable than earlier this year. The combination of moderating rents and improving financing conditions should underpin both the housing market and client financial confidence as we look toward the end of 2025.
