FARE Act Impact

The Fairness in Apartment Rental Expenses (FARE) Act took effect June 11, 2025, shifting broker fee responsibility from renters to landlords. Many landlords have responded by raising rents on new leases to offset these costs, contributing to June's record-high median rent.

Economic Overview

Labor Market Shows Clear Deceleration

Evidence of economic slowdown mounted in June as the Labor Department's May jobs report revealed payroll growth downshifting dramatically. Only 139,000 jobs were added in May, roughly half the pace of early spring. More concerning, revisions showed that hiring in March and April was 95,000 jobs lower than previously estimated.

The unemployment rate held at 4.2%, but this stability masks underlying weakness—over 600,000 Americans left the labor force amid waning confidence. Businesses are growing increasingly cautious on hiring, citing uncertainty from trade policies and tariff volatility.

Federal Reserve Navigates "Stagflation" Concerns

The Federal Reserve maintained its wait-and-see approach, holding rates steady at 4.25%-4.50% for the second consecutive meeting. Fed Chair Jerome Powell acknowledged that tariffs have not yet "shown up" in backward-looking data but warned they could boost inflation in coming months.

May's CPI came in at 2.4% year-over-year, up slightly from April's 2.3%, with core inflation at 2.8%. The Fed now faces the delicate challenge of balancing inflation risks from tariffs against clear signs of economic cooling—a classic "stagflation" scenario that complicates policy decisions.

NYC Real Estate Analysis

Rental Market Reaches New Heights

Manhattan's median rent hit $4,625 in June—yet another record high and the fourth time in five months. The year-over-year increase of 7.6% underscores the persistent strength of rental demand despite affordability challenges.

Competition remains fierce with approximately 25% of Manhattan apartments renting above asking price through bidding wars. The implementation of the FARE Act on June 11 added a new dynamic, as landlords adjusted pricing strategies to account for broker fee obligations.

Local Economy Shows Mixed Signals

NYC's unemployment rate improved to 4.7%, and private-sector payrolls remain near record highs. However, job growth in the first half of 2025 has been the slowest (excluding the pandemic) since 2009, suggesting the city is not immune to national economic headwinds.

The sales market continues to show resilience, with Manhattan co-op and condo median sale prices holding steady year-over-year. Buyer demand remains solid for well-priced homes, supported by mortgage rates that, while elevated, are slightly lower than 2024 peaks.

Market Outlook

June ended with growing consensus that the economy has reached an inflection point. The Federal Reserve's updated projections still show two rate cuts likely by the end of 2025, reflecting mounting economic risks. Markets have pulled back expectations for an early rate cut, now pricing in policy easing only in the fall.

Implications for NYC Real Estate

For Renters: While mortgage rates remain elevated with the Fed on hold, relief could be on the horizon if economic softness prompts late-year rate cuts. The rental market, while historically expensive, may begin to stabilize once the broker-fee law's impact normalizes.

For Buyers: The prospect of future rate cuts should provide some optimism, though timing remains uncertain. Well-priced properties continue to attract solid demand.

Key Factors to Watch:

  • Whether tariff-driven inflation materializes as expected
  • The pace of labor market cooling through summer
  • Fed policy decisions at upcoming meetings
  • Adjustment period for FARE Act implementation
